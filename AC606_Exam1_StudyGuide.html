Key Concepts — Flashcards

Filter by chapter, then click any card to flip between term and definition. Click again to flip back.

Ch. 1 — Services
Auditing vs. Attestation vs. Assurance
Click to reveal
Assurance is the broadest category. Attestation is a subset — the practitioner issues a written conclusion on a written assertion. Auditing is the most specific: a type of attestation focused on financial statements.
Ch. 1 — Audit Risk
Audit Risk
Click to reveal
The risk that an auditor issues an inappropriate opinion on financial statements that are materially misstated. AR = IR × CR × DR. Auditors reduce audit risk to an acceptably low level — not zero.
Ch. 1 — Materiality
Materiality
Click to reveal
A misstatement is material if it would change or influence the judgment of a reasonable user. Involves both quantitative (dollar amount) and qualitative (nature of the item) factors. Largely a matter of professional judgment.
Ch. 1 — Assertions
Management Assertions
Click to reveal
Implicit or explicit claims management makes in the financial statements. Categories: existence, completeness, rights & obligations, accuracy, cut-off, classification, presentation. Auditors design procedures to test each relevant assertion.
Ch. 2 — Regulation
PCAOB vs. AICPA / ASB
Click to reveal
PCAOB: sets auditing standards for public company audits (created by SOX 2002). AICPA / ASB: sets auditing standards for private company audits via Statements on Auditing Standards (SAS). Both issue professional guidance auditors must follow.
Ch. 2 — Independence
Independence in Fact vs. Appearance
Click to reveal
In fact: the auditor actually maintains an unbiased mental attitude. In appearance: a reasonable, informed third party would conclude the auditor is independent. Both are required — always. There are no exceptions.
Ch. 2 — Governance
Corporate Governance & the Audit Committee
Click to reveal
The board of directors oversees management on behalf of shareholders. The audit committee (independent directors) oversees financial reporting and the external audit. Under SOX, the audit committee — not management — hires and oversees the external auditor.
Ch. 2 — Standards
Professional Guidance & Due Care
Click to reveal
Auditors follow a hierarchy of professional guidance: PCAOB standards (public) or GAAS / SAS (private), plus the AICPA Code of Professional Conduct for ethics. Due professional care requires exercising professional skepticism throughout the entire audit.
Ch. 2 — Responsibility
Management vs. Auditor Responsibility
Click to reveal
Management bears ultimate responsibility for the financial statements. The auditor is responsible for expressing an opinion on whether those statements are fairly presented. The auditor provides reasonable — not absolute — assurance.
Ch. 3 — Planning
Engagement Letter
Click to reveal
A formal written agreement documenting the terms of the engagement before work begins. Includes: scope, responsibilities of each party, fee arrangements, form of the report, and limitations. Establishes mutual understanding between auditor and client.
Ch. 3 — Planning
Client Acceptance & Continuance
Click to reveal
Before accepting or continuing with a client, the auditor evaluates management integrity, auditor competence, independence, and engagement risk. If a prospective client refuses predecessor-auditor communication, the successor should have serious reservations about accepting.
Ch. 3 — Tests
Substantive Testing
Click to reveal
Audit procedures designed to detect material misstatements directly at the assertion level. Two types: (1) Tests of details — examine individual transactions or balances. (2) Substantive analytical procedures — compare recorded amounts to expectations developed from other data.
Ch. 3 — Tests
Dual-Purpose Test
Click to reveal
A single procedure that simultaneously serves as both a test of controls AND a substantive test of transactions. Example: inspecting an invoice for proper authorization (control) AND verifying the dollar amount matches the journal entry (substantive). Efficient use of audit resources.
Ch. 3 — Materiality
Performance Materiality
Click to reveal
Also called tolerable misstatement. Set lower than overall planning materiality at the account or assertion level. Purpose: reduce the risk that the aggregate of individually immaterial uncorrected misstatements exceeds overall planning materiality.
Ch. 4 — Risk Model
Inherent Risk
Click to reveal
The susceptibility of an assertion to material misstatement assuming no related controls exist. Driven by the complexity or nature of the account, industry, or transaction. The auditor assesses inherent risk but cannot change it.
Ch. 4 — Risk Model
Detection Risk
Click to reveal
The risk that audit procedures will not detect a material misstatement that exists. The only component the auditor directly controls — by adjusting the nature, timing, and extent of substantive procedures. Varies inversely with inherent and control risk.
Ch. 4 — Fraud
The Fraud Triangle
Click to reveal
Three conditions typically present when fraud occurs: Incentive/Pressure (motivation), Opportunity (weak controls or oversight), and Rationalization (ability to justify the act). All three often coexist in fraud scenarios.
Ch. 4 — Risk
Engagement Risk
Click to reveal
The risk to the auditor from association with a client — reputational, legal, or financial. Unlike audit risk, engagement risk cannot be eliminated. It can only be managed through careful client acceptance and continuance policies.
Ch. 5 — Evidence
Sufficiency vs. Appropriateness
Click to reveal
Sufficiency = the quantity of evidence (enough to support the conclusion). Appropriateness = the quality (relevance + reliability). The auditor must satisfy both dimensions. More evidence is not always better if it lacks quality.
Ch. 5 — Evidence
Audit Evidence Reliability
Click to reveal
Evidence is more reliable when: from independent external sources; obtained directly by the auditor; in documentary form; from an entity with effective controls. Inquiry (oral) alone is the least reliable form of evidence.
Ch. 5 — Evidence
Vouching vs. Tracing
Click to reveal
Vouching: recorded entry → source document. Tests occurrence/existence (did it actually happen?). Tracing: source document → recorded entry. Tests completeness (was everything recorded?). Direction matters.
Ch. 5 — Documentation
Current File vs. Permanent File
Click to reveal
Current file: current-year specific items (lead schedules, adjusting entries, current-year audit report, confirmations). Permanent file: information relevant across multiple years (chart of accounts, articles of incorporation, debt agreements, prior audited financials).
Ch. 5 — Documentation
Purpose of Working Papers
Click to reveal
Working papers support the audit opinion, document that standards were followed, facilitate review and supervision of audit staff, and provide a basis for future audits. They are the property of the auditor — not the client, shareholders, or SEC.

1  |  AICPA Conceptual Framework for Members in Public Practice

+

The AICPA Conceptual Framework is a principles-based approach that guides members in identifying, evaluating, and responding to threats to compliance with the fundamental ethical principles. Know all five steps, especially the final documentation step.

1
Identify the threat. Recognize any condition, circumstance, or relationship that could compromise compliance with the fundamental principles: objectivity, integrity, independence, professional competence, confidentiality, and professional behavior.
2
Evaluate the significance of the threat. Consider both the likelihood and the potential magnitude. Is the threat already at an acceptable level, or does it require further action?
3
Identify and apply safeguards. Safeguards may come from the profession (standards, licensing requirements), the client (governance structures, audit committees), or the firm (rotation policies, second-partner review, consultation). Apply safeguards to eliminate or reduce the threat to an acceptable level.
4
Evaluate the residual threat. After safeguards are applied, assess whether the remaining threat is acceptably low. If not, the member must decline or discontinue the engagement.
5
Document the analysis. This is the final — and most important — step. The member must document the threats identified, the evaluation process used, the safeguards applied, and the conclusion reached. Documentation creates an evidence trail that demonstrates professional judgment was exercised and the framework was properly followed. Without documentation, the analysis may as well not have happened.

The Five Threat Categories:

  • Self-interest — a financial or other interest that inappropriately influences judgment (e.g., a contingent fee tied to a favorable outcome)
  • Self-review — evaluating your own prior work (e.g., auditing financial statements the firm helped prepare)
  • Advocacy — promoting a client's position to the point that objectivity is compromised
  • Familiarity — a close relationship with the client leads to excessive trust or sympathy
  • Intimidation — actual or perceived pressure from the client or management
For the essay: use the five-step structure to walk through the given scenario. Identify the specific threat type, explain its significance, describe the safeguard you would apply, evaluate whether it reduces the threat sufficiently, and emphasize that everything must be documented. If no safeguard is sufficient, the member must withdraw.

2  |  Professional Skepticism

+

Professional skepticism is an attitude that includes a questioning mind and a critical assessment of audit evidence. It is required by both GAAS and PCAOB standards as part of due professional care.

What professional skepticism is:

  • A questioning mind — not automatically accepting management's explanations or representations
  • Critical assessment of audit evidence for sufficiency and appropriateness
  • Heightened alertness to conditions that may indicate fraud, error, or deliberate misstatement
  • Willingness to challenge representations that seem inconsistent with other evidence

Why it matters:

  • Required by GAAS and PCAOB as part of due professional care
  • Reduces the risk that auditors are misled by management or accept insufficient evidence
  • Essential to detecting fraud, which often involves intentional concealment of information
  • Protects the public interest and maintains the credibility and integrity of the audit function

How it is applied:

  • Corroborating management explanations with independent, external evidence rather than taking them at face value
  • Conducting brainstorming sessions among the audit team at engagement start to discuss how fraud might occur at this client
  • Recognizing and managing cognitive biases (anchoring, confirmation bias) that can quietly undermine skeptical thinking
  • Actively evaluating evidence that contradicts prior conclusions, not just evidence that confirms them
  • Applying heightened scrutiny when fraud risk factors from the fraud triangle are present
Key distinction: professional skepticism does not mean assuming management is dishonest. It means the auditor does not assume management is honest without corroborating evidence. The auditor maintains an open, questioning mind and lets the evidence lead the conclusion.

3  |  Materiality

+

Materiality is the magnitude of an omission or misstatement that, in light of the surrounding circumstances, makes it probable that the judgment of a reasonable person relying on the financial statements would have been changed or influenced.

Why materiality is important to an audit:

  • Auditors cannot test everything — materiality helps them focus effort on what matters most to financial statement users
  • Determines which misstatements must be corrected or disclosed before an unmodified opinion can be issued
  • Directly influences the nature, timing, and extent of audit procedures throughout the engagement
  • Provides the basis for evaluating whether the aggregate of uncorrected misstatements is acceptable

Quantitative vs. Qualitative:

  • Quantitative — dollar thresholds (e.g., 5% of pre-tax income, 0.5% of total assets). These are starting points, not bright-line rules.
  • Qualitative — the nature of the item can make even a small misstatement material: illegal payments, items that turn a profit into a loss, misstatements that affect regulatory compliance, or items involving key management personnel.
  • Materiality is largely a matter of professional judgment and must always consider both dimensions

Planning vs. Performance Materiality:

  • Planning materiality — set at the overall financial statement level at the start of the audit
  • Performance materiality (tolerable misstatement) — set lower, at the account or assertion level, to protect against the risk that individually immaterial misstatements aggregate above planning materiality
Always discuss both the quantitative and qualitative dimensions. Emphasize that materiality is a matter of professional judgment — quantitative benchmarks are a starting point, not the final answer.

4  |  Phases of the Audit

+

A financial statement audit follows a structured sequence of phases. You should be able to identify, describe, and explain the purpose of each — not just name it.

  • Phase 1 — Client Acceptance & Continuance: Evaluate management integrity, auditor independence and competence, and engagement risk. Communicate with the predecessor auditor for new clients. Issue the engagement letter to document the agreed-upon terms before work begins.
  • Phase 2 — Preliminary Engagement Activities: Confirm independence and ethical compliance. Establish mutual understanding of engagement terms. Assemble the engagement team and assign responsibilities appropriately.
  • Phase 3 — Plan the Audit: Develop the overall audit strategy. Set planning and performance materiality. Perform risk assessment procedures — obtain a thorough understanding of the client, its environment, and its internal control structure.
  • Phase 4 — Consider & Evaluate Internal Controls: Assess control risk. Test controls if the auditor plans to rely on them. Identify control deficiencies, significant deficiencies, and material weaknesses. Communicate findings to management and those charged with governance.
  • Phase 5 — Substantive Procedures: Perform tests of details and substantive analytical procedures at the assertion level to detect material misstatements that internal controls did not prevent or detect.
  • Phase 6 — Complete the Audit: Perform final analytical procedures. Evaluate all audit findings. Assess going concern. Review for subsequent events. Obtain the management representation letter. Communicate required matters to those charged with governance.
  • Phase 7 — Issue the Audit Report: Form the audit opinion and issue the appropriate report — unmodified, qualified, adverse, or disclaimer of opinion.
Know the purpose of each phase, not just its name. A strong essay will explain what happens in each phase and why it matters to the overall integrity of the audit.
